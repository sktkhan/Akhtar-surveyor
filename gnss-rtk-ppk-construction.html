GNSS in Construction Surveying:
RTK, PPK and When to Use Each

GPS changed surveying. GNSS changed it further. But standing in a construction site with a receiver telling you "float solution — accuracy 0.45 m" is useless. Understanding RTK, PPK, and the limits of each method turns a black box into a reliable professional tool.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 12 min read Technology

GNSS — Global Navigation Satellite System — is the umbrella term for all satellite positioning systems. GPS is just one of them (the American one). When a construction surveyor sets up a rover receiver today, it is simultaneously tracking satellites from multiple constellations, using signals from dozens of satellites at once. This multi-constellation capability is what makes modern GNSS receivers so much more reliable than the GPS receivers of twenty years ago.

But raw GNSS — just a receiver watching satellites — gives you position accuracy of 2–5 metres. That is useless for engineering. The solution is differential GNSS: comparing the receiver's observations against a reference point of known position and using the difference to correct the errors. RTK and PPK are the two most important forms of differential GNSS for construction surveyors.

How GNSS Positioning Works

Every GNSS satellite broadcasts a signal containing two pieces of information: its precise position (from the satellite's own onboard clock and ephemeris data) and the exact time the signal was transmitted. The receiver measures how long the signal takes to arrive. Multiply by the speed of light and you have the distance from that satellite.

With distances from four or more satellites, the receiver can solve for its own position (E, N, H) and correct its clock error simultaneously. This is trilateration — not triangulation. The result is a pseudorange position — "pseudo" because the ranges contain errors from the ionosphere, troposphere, satellite clock drift and receiver noise.

Pseudorange and Error Sources
Pseudorange = True range + Clock errors + Ionospheric delay + Tropospheric delay + Multipath + Noise Raw GNSS accuracy: ±2–5 m horizontal, ±5–10 m vertical With SBAS correction: ±0.5–1.5 m horizontal With RTK (fixed): ±8–15 mm horizontal, ±15–25 mm vertical
The dominant error for construction surveying is the ionospheric delay — the signal slows as it passes through the ionosphere and the delay varies with solar activity, time of day and location. Differential GNSS methods cancel most of this error by comparing two receivers that experience very similar atmospheric delays.
"The atmosphere does not care about your project deadline. GNSS accuracy is ultimately constrained by physics — understanding the limits protects you from trusting results you should not trust."

The Differential Correction Principle

The key insight behind all high-accuracy GNSS methods is that errors are spatially correlated. Two receivers that are close together experience almost identical atmospheric delays, satellite clock errors and orbit errors. If one receiver is on a known point, it can calculate the errors in its own position and broadcast corrections. A nearby rover receiver applies those same corrections to its own pseudoranges — and most of the systematic errors cancel out.

The closer the rover is to the base, the better the error cancellation. This is why base-rover GNSS systems have a recommended baseline length (typically under 20–30 km for RTK, though network RTK extends this considerably).

RTK — Real-Time Kinematic Explained

RTK is differential GNSS where the corrections are transmitted from the base station to the rover in real time — via a radio link (UHF/VHF) or an internet connection (NTRIP over mobile data). The rover applies the corrections and solves for its position in real time, typically within 1–5 seconds of switching on.

Base-Rover Architecture

In a classic own-base RTK setup:

  1. The base station is set up over a known control point (or at an assumed position if doing a relative survey). It observes satellites and transmits corrections continuously
  2. The rover receives the corrections via radio or internet, combines them with its own satellite observations, and computes a differential position in real time
  3. The rover operator works normally — walking to points, collecting data, or guiding setting-out — while the system provides continuous centimetre-level position updates
Fig 1. RTK base-rover architecture. The base station on a known point transmits satellite observation corrections via radio or internet to the rover. The rover applies the corrections and computes a fixed solution in real time. The baseline (base-to-rover distance) should ideally be under 20 km for best results.

Fixed vs Float Solution — Critical Distinction

✓ Fixed Solution

Centimetre Accuracy

The receiver has resolved the carrier phase integer ambiguity — it knows exactly how many full wavelengths of signal are between the satellite and receiver. This is the goal of all RTK work.

Typical horizontal accuracy:

±8–15 mm
⚠ Float Solution

Decimetre Accuracy Only

The receiver has not resolved the integer ambiguity — it has only estimated it. The position is computed but the uncertainty is much larger. A float solution is not acceptable for engineering setting out or precise survey work.

Typical horizontal accuracy:

±0.2–0.5 m
Never Accept a Float Solution for Engineering Work

The most dangerous situation in GNSS surveying is collecting data on a float solution thinking it is fixed. Always confirm the solution status before every observation series. If you are working near obstructions (trees, buildings, slopes) the solution can drop from fixed to float and back — check constantly. A 0.3 m float error in a pile position or road alignment can cause expensive rework.

Network RTK (NRTK / VRS)

Setting up your own base station takes time and requires a known control point near the site. Network RTK eliminates the base station entirely — instead, you subscribe to a network of permanent reference stations operated by a national or commercial provider, and receive corrections over the internet (NTRIP protocol).

  • The network creates a Virtual Reference Station (VRS) — a mathematically synthesised base station at a point close to your rover's approximate position
  • Corrections from the VRS are delivered to your rover via a mobile data connection (4G/5G)
  • Accuracy is similar to own-base RTK: ±10–20 mm horizontal when fixed
  • No base equipment to set up, transport or power — one receiver, one person, immediate start
  • Requires a mobile data signal — fails in remote areas or underground
  • Requires a subscription (typically charged annually or per-month)
  • The coordinate system delivered by the network may differ slightly from your site control — always calibrate against local control before trusting absolute coordinates

PPK — Post-Processed Kinematic

Real-Time

RTK — Instant Results

Corrections applied as you work. Position displayed in real time. Ideal for setting out, topographic survey and most construction work where immediate feedback is needed.

  • Results available immediately on site
  • Requires radio or internet link to base
  • If link drops, survey stops
  • No re-processing possible if solution was float
Post-Processed

PPK — Highest Reliability

Raw observations stored in both base and rover. Processed after the survey using specialist software. The integer ambiguity can be resolved retrospectively — including sections where the real-time link was lost.

  • No real-time link required in the field
  • Can recover data from obstructed areas
  • Results only available after processing
  • Best for UAV photogrammetry, remote surveys

When PPK is the Better Choice

  • UAV (drone) surveys: The drone cannot maintain a radio link to a base station during flight. PPK processes the flight's raw observations against a simultaneously logging base station after landing — giving centimetre-level geotagging of every photo
  • Remote or obstructed sites: No mobile data, radio range too short for RTK. PPK logs raw data and processes it back at the office against data from the nearest continuously operating reference station (CORS)
  • Quality assurance: Even when RTK is used on site, logging raw PPK data simultaneously gives you a backup that can be reprocessed if there were doubts about the real-time solution quality
  • Absolute accuracy requirements: When you need to demonstrate traceability to a national reference frame, PPK processing against published CORS data provides the clearest audit trail

Vertical Accuracy is Always Worse Than Horizontal

GNSS satellites are above the horizon — there are no satellites below you. This means the vertical component of position is geometrically weaker than horizontal. As a rule of thumb, vertical accuracy is typically 1.5–3× worse than horizontal accuracy. For precise level setting out (drainage inverts, formation levels, slab levels), always verify GNSS-derived heights against a spirit level run from a benchmarked control point.

GNSS vs Total Station — The Decision Guide

📡 Use GNSS When:

Open Sky, Large Area, Fast Coverage

  • Open site with good sky view (PDOP < 3.0)
  • Large topographic surveys — faster to cover with rover than total station
  • Control network establishment over large distances
  • UAV ground control points (GCPs) and PPK tagging
  • Earthworks progress surveys — speed more important than 5 mm precision
  • Road centreline pegging on long alignments in open country
  • One-person operation when you cannot have an assistant for the prism
🔭 Use Total Station When:

Obstructions, Precision, Enclosed Spaces

  • Under tree canopy, near buildings or in urban canyons
  • Inside structures — tunnels, basements, building frames
  • Precise structural setting out (columns, piles) requiring ±5 mm
  • Precise level setting — drain inverts, slab levels, road formation
  • Areas where multipath is high (near large metal structures)
  • Monitoring surveys requiring sub-millimetre repeatability
  • When working to a site grid with a tight coordinate system

In practice, most professional survey teams use both. GNSS for broad area coverage and control, total station for precise detail and setting out in constrained areas. The tools are complementary — the skill is knowing which to reach for.

Practical Site Tips for RTK GNSS

01
Always calibrate against local control before starting

Observe at least two known control points at the start of each session. Compare the GNSS-derived coordinates with the known values. If the discrepancy exceeds your project tolerance, do not proceed — the network correction may be offset from your site grid, or the base is on the wrong coordinates.

02
Check PDOP before collecting data

PDOP (Position Dilution of Precision) measures satellite geometry. PDOP under 2.0 is excellent; under 3.0 is acceptable; above 4.0 is marginal. Most receivers display PDOP continuously. Plan surveys during periods of good geometry — many satellite planning apps let you predict PDOP by location and time of day.

03
Initialise (fix) in open sky, not under trees

Getting a fixed RTK solution requires unobstructed satellite signals. Initialise in the most open part of the site, then move into the work area. Once fixed, the receiver can maintain the solution in moderate obstruction — but it cannot fix for the first time in a degraded environment.

04
Take multiple observations of important points

For setting out or picking up control points, take 3–5 observations with 5–10 second intervals and average them. This reduces the effect of residual noise and gives you a standard deviation on the result. Any observation with residual over 15 mm should be discarded and repeated.

05
Keep the pole vertical — always

The GNSS antenna position is computed at the top of the pole. A 2 m pole that is 2° from vertical introduces a 70 mm horizontal error at the ground mark. Use the pole bubble and confirm it before every observation. On windy sites, shield the pole with your body or use a bipod support.

06
Re-check solution status after any obstruction

Walking under a tree, past a building or into a cutting can cause the solution to drop to float. After emerging from an obstruction, wait for the fixed light to re-confirm before collecting data. Add a 10-second buffer after re-fixing before recording any point.

Common GNSS Mistakes on Construction Sites

  • Treating float as fixed: The most costly error. Ensure the receiver display clearly shows "FIXED" — not "FLOAT," "DGNSS," or "AUTONOMOUS" — before every observation
  • No local calibration check: Assuming the NRTK network is exactly aligned with the site coordinate system. Always verify against known control at the start of every session and after any equipment change
  • Wrong antenna height: The antenna height (pole height + phase centre offset) must be entered correctly. An error of 10 mm propagates to every level you measure. Measure pole height with a tape, not by memory
  • Multipath from site equipment: Setting up the base receiver next to a steel hoarding, container or parked machine. The reflected signals create multipath errors. Base should be in the open, away from reflective surfaces
  • Using GNSS heights as spot levels: GNSS heights are ellipsoidal — referenced to the mathematical ellipsoid, not to mean sea level. The difference (the geoid undulation) can be 10–50 m. Always apply the correct geoid model or calibrate heights to local benchmarks before using for level control
  • Relying on GNSS in multipath-heavy environments: Around large warehouses, under power lines, near retaining walls — GNSS can give a fixed solution that is still 50–100 mm wrong due to multipath. Verify with a total station check in these conditions

Summary

GNSS is one of the most powerful tools in modern construction surveying — and one of the easiest to misuse. The difference between a fixed RTK solution (±10 mm) and a float solution (±400 mm) is invisible on the receiver display unless you know to check. The difference between ellipsoidal height and orthometric height is invisible unless you know to apply a geoid model. The multipath error that makes your fixed solution wrong by 80 mm is invisible unless you verify against a total station.

RTK is the workhorse for fast open-area surveying. PPK is the choice when reliability matters more than real-time feedback — UAV surveys, remote sites, quality assurance logging. Network RTK eliminates the base station and is now the standard for most professional daily survey work where mobile data is available.

Use GNSS for speed and coverage. Use a total station for precision and enclosed spaces. Verify both against known control points. Understand the limitations of every method you use — and your survey work will be both fast and reliable.
