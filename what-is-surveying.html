What is Surveying?
A Complete Beginner's Guide

Whether you are a student stepping into engineering or just curious about how bridges, roads and buildings are planned — this guide explains land surveying in plain, simple English.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 12 min read Beginner Level

Imagine you are about to build a house. You have the land, you have the money, and you have an architect's drawing. But before a single brick is laid, someone needs to come out to that piece of land and measure it precisely — its size, shape, levels, and exact position on the earth. That person is a surveyor, and what they do is called surveying.

Surveying is one of the oldest professions in human history. The ancient Egyptians used it to re-establish land boundaries after the Nile flooded every year. The Romans used it to lay perfectly straight roads across entire continents. And today, without modern surveying, we could not build roads, railways, tunnels, bridges, dams, or even map our own cities accurately.

Simple Definition

Surveying is the science and art of measuring the earth's surface — determining positions, distances, angles, and elevations — to create maps, lay out construction projects, and establish boundaries.

Why is Surveying So Important?

Before anything gets built, surveying happens first. And when construction is complete, surveying checks that everything was built in the right place. It is the foundation of all civil engineering work — quite literally.

Here are some everyday situations that completely depend on accurate surveying:

  • Building your house in the correct boundary — not on your neighbour's land
  • Designing a road that does not flood because the levels are wrong
  • Tunnelling through a mountain from both sides and meeting exactly in the middle
  • Installing irrigation channels at the right slope so water flows correctly
  • Building a bridge that lines up perfectly with the roads on both banks
  • Creating accurate Google Maps and satellite navigation systems
"Every building you see, every road you drive on, and every map you use — they all started with a surveyor holding an instrument and taking a measurement."

From land boundaries and road alignment to building set-out and topographic mapping — surveying underpins every stage of construction.

Types of Surveying

Surveying covers a huge range of activities. Depending on the purpose, surveyors specialise in different types. Here are the most important ones you will come across:

1. Land (Cadastral) Surveying

This is the type most people have heard of. A cadastral surveyor measures and records the legal boundaries of land. When you buy a plot of land, a land surveyor defines exactly where your boundary is and produces an official plan. This is also used for subdivisions — splitting one large piece of land into smaller plots.

2. Topographic Surveying

A topographic survey measures the shape of the ground — hills, valleys, slopes, rivers, trees and existing structures. The result is a detailed topographic map with contour lines showing the rise and fall of the land. Engineers use these maps to design roads, drainage systems, and buildings.

3. Engineering (Construction) Surveying

This is what you might call field surveying. Engineering surveyors set out the position of roads, foundations, pipelines, and structures — marking exactly where each element should be built according to the design drawings. This requires high precision and constant fieldwork.

4. Route Surveying

Used for linear projects like roads, railways, canals and pipelines. Route surveying determines the best path for the route, surveys the existing ground along that path, and helps engineers design the horizontal and vertical alignment — including curves, gradients and embankments.

5. Hydrographic Surveying

Conducted on and below the water — measuring the depth, shape and features of rivers, lakes, harbours and the seabed. This data is used for nautical charts, port design, underwater pipelines and coastal engineering.

6. Geodetic Surveying

This is large-scale, high-precision surveying that accounts for the curvature of the earth. Used for national coordinate systems, satellite positioning networks (like GPS), and projects spanning hundreds of kilometres.

Surveying Instruments — The Tools of the Trade

Surveyors use a range of specialised instruments. Over the centuries these have evolved from simple chains and compasses to laser-guided digital equipment. Here are the main ones you should know:

🎯
Total Station
The main modern surveying instrument. Measures angles and distances electronically.
⚖️
Dumpy Level
Used for levelling — measuring height differences between points.
📡
GPS / GNSS
Satellite-based positioning. Used for large areas and mapping.
📐
Theodolite
Measures horizontal and vertical angles precisely. Older but still used.
📏

A graduated rod used with a level to measure height differences.
✈️

Modern aerial surveying tool for large areas, terrain mapping and inspections.

The Total Station — A Closer Look

If you work in construction or civil engineering, the Total Station is the instrument you will use most. It sits on a tripod at a known point and can measure horizontal angles, vertical angles, and distances simultaneously — all in one shot. The measurements are stored digitally and can be downloaded to a computer for calculation and drawing.

Good to Know

A modern Total Station with GPS connectivity is called a Robotic Total Station — it can track a prism pole carried by one person, while the other person stays at the instrument. One person can do the work of a two-man crew.

The Three Fundamental Measurements

No matter what type of surveying you are doing, everything comes back to three basic measurements. Understand these and you understand the core of surveying.

01
Distance

The straight-line length between two points, measured in metres. Modern total stations measure distance using an invisible laser beam bounced off a prism reflector — accurate to within a few millimetres over hundreds of metres.

02
Angle (Bearing)

The direction of a line, measured in degrees from North. In professional surveying we use Whole Circle Bearing (WCB) — a value from 0° to 360°. Angles are also expressed in DMS format — Degrees, Minutes, Seconds. For example: 47° 23' 15".

03
Elevation (Reduced Level)

The height of a point above a reference datum — usually Mean Sea Level (MSL). In levelling, we calculate Reduced Levels (RL) which tell us how high or low each point is. This is critical for drainage design, floor levels, road gradients and flood risk assessment.

Key Surveying Terms You Should Know

Like any profession, surveying has its own vocabulary. Here are the most important terms explained simply:

  • Benchmark (BM) — A fixed point of known elevation, used as a reference for levelling. Permanent benchmarks are marked on buildings or set in concrete by government agencies.
  • Traverse — A series of connected survey lines forming a network of points. Used to map large areas or establish control points.
  • Bearing — The direction of a survey line, measured as an angle from North. Whole Circle Bearing (WCB) ranges from 0° to 360°.
  • Chainage — The running distance along a road, railway or pipeline from a fixed starting point. For example, chainage 1+450 means 1,450 metres from the start.
  • Offset — The perpendicular distance from a reference line to a point or feature. Used in road surveying and setting out.
  • Control Point — A fixed, precisely measured point used as the basis for all other measurements on a project.
  • Setting Out — The process of marking the position of engineering works (foundations, roads, drains) on the ground based on design drawings.
  • Contour Line — A line on a map connecting all points at the same elevation. Closely spaced contours = steep ground. Widely spaced = gentle slope.
  • Misclosure — The small error in a survey loop when you measure back to your starting point. A measure of survey accuracy.
  • Coordinate (E, N) — The position of a point defined by its Easting (E) and Northing (N) values in a coordinate grid system.

How a Survey Actually Works — Step by Step

Let's say a new road needs to be built. Here is how a surveyor approaches the job from start to finish:

01
Reconnaissance (Site Visit)

Before any measurements are taken, the surveyor walks the site to understand the terrain, identify existing features, locate existing control points, and plan the safest and most efficient survey approach.

02
Control Network Setup

A network of precisely measured control points is established across the site using GPS or total station. These become the reference framework for all other measurements — like the skeleton of the survey.

03
Detail Survey

The surveyor measures every relevant feature — ground levels, existing roads, buildings, trees, fences, drainage channels. All data is recorded digitally in the total station's memory.

04
Data Processing

Back in the office, the raw measurements are downloaded and processed using specialist software. Coordinates and levels are calculated, adjusted for any errors, and used to create drawings and reports.

05
Setting Out

Once the engineer has designed the road, the surveyor returns to site to mark its exact position on the ground — placing pegs, nails and marks at critical points like the centreline, edge of road, and structure positions.

06
As-Built Survey

After construction, the surveyor checks that everything was built in the right position and at the right levels — producing an "as-built" record of exactly what was built and where.

Understanding Coordinates in Surveying

One of the most fundamental concepts in surveying is the coordinate system. Instead of describing a location in words, we use numbers — specifically Easting (E) and Northing (N) values.

Think of it like a graph. The Easting is the horizontal position (left-right), and the Northing is the vertical position (up-down). Together they pinpoint a location exactly. Every survey point, every building corner, every road centreline — it all has an E and N coordinate.

The coordinate system used globally is called UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator). It divides the world into 60 numbered zones, each with its own grid. GPS devices and surveying instruments all work in UTM or a related system. You can use the Lat/Long to UTM Calculator on this website to convert between geographic coordinates and UTM.

Common Beginner Mistake

Confusing Easting and Northing with Longitude and Latitude. They are related but different. Easting/Northing are flat grid coordinates (in metres). Latitude/Longitude are angular coordinates on the curved earth (in degrees). Always check which system your instruments and software are using.

How to Become a Land Surveyor

Surveying is a rewarding career with strong job prospects, good pay, and a lot of variety — you spend time outdoors in the field and in the office doing analysis and design. Here is how most surveyors get started:

  • Study Civil Engineering, Geomatics, or a dedicated Land Surveying degree
  • Learn to operate a Total Station and Level in the field — hands-on experience is everything
  • Understand coordinate geometry, trigonometry, and basic calculus
  • Learn CAD software (AutoCAD Civil 3D is the industry standard) for drawing from survey data
  • Complete field placements or internships with a surveying company
  • Study for professional membership (RICS, Institution of Civil Engineers, etc.)
  • Keep learning — technology changes fast in this field (drones, LiDAR, BIM)

Modern Surveying — Where Technology is Heading

The profession has changed enormously in the last 20 years, and the pace of change is only accelerating. Here is what modern surveying looks like today:

🚁
Drone Surveying (UAV)
A drone with a camera can survey hundreds of hectares in hours, producing accurate 3D point clouds and orthophoto maps.
⚡
LiDAR Scanning
Laser scanning creates millions of 3D points per second — producing a precise digital twin of any building, tunnel or landscape.
🛰️
RTK GPS
Real-Time Kinematic GPS gives centimetre-accurate positions in seconds — transforming fieldwork speed and reducing manpower needs.
🏛️
BIM Integration
Survey data feeds directly into Building Information Models — connecting field reality to design and construction management.

Conclusion

Surveying is much more than just "measuring land." It is the precise science that makes the physical world possible — connecting the lines on a drawing to the concrete reality of roads, buildings and infrastructure that we use every day.

Whether you are a student, a construction professional looking to understand the tools around you, or someone who just bought a piece of land — understanding the basics of surveying will serve you well. The measurements are precise. The mathematics is logical. And the results are visible everywhere you look.
