Vertical Curves:
Sag vs Crest and the K-Value Explained

Every hill, valley and grade change on a road is designed using a parabolic vertical curve. Understanding how to calculate the curve length, check sight distance and determine levels at any point is essential for any road engineer — and it is simpler than it looks.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 12 min read Intermediate

Horizontal curves get a lot of attention — but the vertical alignment is just as critical. A road that rises, falls and changes grade abruptly is uncomfortable to drive, reduces sight distance, and can hide hazards. The answer is the parabolic vertical curve: a smooth, gradual transition between two grades that is safe, aesthetically clean, and straightforward to calculate once you know the method.

This article covers both types — crest curves (over hills) and sag curves (in valleys) — with the K-value system used to select curve lengths, the key formulas, and two complete worked examples including a full level table.

What This Article Covers

Crest vs sag curve definitions — parabolic curve geometry (PVI, PVC, PVT, L, A, K, e) — the K-value and standard tables — stopping sight distance formulas — crest worked example with level table — sag worked example — and the high/low point formula.

What is a Vertical Curve?

A vertical curve is a section of road profile that transitions smoothly between two straight grades. The two straight sections are called tangent grades — expressed as percentages (e.g., +3%, −2%). The point where the two tangent grades meet (if extended) is the Point of Vertical Intersection (PVI), also called the grade point or IP.

Just as a horizontal curve connects two tangent directions, a vertical curve connects two grade tangents. And just as horizontal curves use a circular arc, vertical curves use a parabola — chosen because its rate of change of grade is constant (the second derivative is constant), which means the rate of change of centripetal acceleration felt by a vehicle is also constant — smooth and predictable.

"The parabola is not just mathematically convenient — it gives the most comfortable ride of any smooth curve because the grade changes at a constant rate throughout."

Crest Curves vs Sag Curves

▲ Crest Curve

Over a Hill

A crest curve occurs when the road rises on the entry grade and falls on the exit grade — or when both grades rise but the exit grade is less steep. The curve is convex — curving downward. The critical design check is stopping sight distance: a driver must be able to see far enough ahead to stop before hitting an obstacle that might be hidden beyond the crest.

🚗 Sight distance critical — headlight range not relevant
▼ Sag Curve

In a Valley

A sag curve occurs when the road falls on the entry grade and rises on the exit grade — or when both grades fall but the exit grade is less steep. The curve is concave — curving upward. The design checks are headlight sight distance (at night, how far headlights illuminate) and riding comfort (centripetal force felt as the vehicle curves upward at the bottom of the valley).

💡 Headlight distance & rider comfort govern
Fig 1. Left: Crest curve — convex, critical for stopping sight distance over the hill. Right: Sag curve — concave, critical for headlight sight distance at night and rider comfort. PVC = start of curve, PVT = end, PVI = grade intersection.

Key Elements and Terminology

  • PVC — Point of Vertical Curvature. Where the curve begins — the end of the entry tangent grade and the start of the parabola.
  • PVT — Point of Vertical Tangency. Where the curve ends — the end of the parabola and the start of the exit tangent grade.
  • PVI — Point of Vertical Intersection. Where the two tangent grades would meet if extended. The PVI is the mid-point of the horizontal projection (chainage of PVI = chainage of PVC + L/2).
  • G1 — Entry grade in percent. Positive = uphill in the direction of increasing chainage; negative = downhill.
  • G2 — Exit grade in percent. Same sign convention as G1.
  • A — Algebraic difference of grades: A = G2 − G1 (in percent). Positive A = sag curve. Negative A = crest curve.
  • L — Length of the vertical curve in metres (horizontal distance from PVC to PVT).
  • K — The K-value: K = L / |A|. Expresses the curve length needed per 1% change in grade. The fundamental design parameter.
  • e — Mid-curve offset: the vertical distance from the PVI down to the curve at the midpoint. e = A × L / 800 (in metres, with A in %, L in m).

The K-Value — The Designer's Tool

The K-value is the single most useful number in vertical curve design. It expresses the curve length per 1% of grade change — so once you know K, finding L for any A is trivial:

The K-Value Relationship
K = L / |A| L = K × |A|
Where L = curve length (m), A = algebraic difference of grades G2−G1 (%), and |A| = absolute value (always positive). K is determined by sight distance requirements — national design standards provide minimum K values for each design speed.

National standards (AASHTO, UK Design Manual, etc.) tabulate minimum K-values for each design speed. Here are the standard values used in most countries — always use the greater of the crest and sag K for your design speed:

Crest K values are much smaller than Sag K values — why?

At a crest, sight distance depends on the geometry of the hill — even daylight gives you limited visibility over a sharp crest. At a sag, in daylight you can see the road ahead clearly regardless of the curve — but at night, your headlights only illuminate a limited distance, which depends on the curve shape. The headlight sight distance requirement produces larger minimum K values for sag curves at high design speeds.

All Formulas — Quick Reference

Parabolic Vertical Curve — Complete Formula Set
Grade difference: A = G2 − G1 (% — can be negative) Minimum length: L = K × |A| (m) Mid-curve offset: e = A × L / 800 (m) RL at any point x: RL_x = RL_PVC + G1·x/100 + (A/200L)·x² High/Low point x: x_HL = −G1 × L / A (m from PVC) K-value: K = L / |A|
x = horizontal distance from PVC in metres. G1 and G2 in %. L in metres. The parabola equation gives the level at any point — use it to build a full level table. The high/low point formula gives where the tangent to the parabola is horizontal (zero grade).
Sign of A tells you the curve type

A = G2 − G1. If A is negative, the exit grade is less than the entry grade — the road grade is decreasing — this is a crest curve. If A is positive, the exit grade exceeds the entry grade — the road is getting steeper uphill or bottoming out — this is a sag curve. A = 0 means no change in grade — no curve needed.

Worked Example 1 — Crest Curve

A road at 80 km/h design speed has a crest at a PVI. The entry grade is G1 = +4% and the exit grade is G2 = −2%. The PVI is at chainage 3+200.000 with RL 65.450 m. Design the vertical curve and calculate levels at 20 m intervals.

Step 1 — Calculate A

A = G2 − G1 = −2 − (+4) = −6% (negative → crest curve ✓)

Step 2 — Minimum Curve Length

From table at 80 km/h: K_crest(min) = 26
L_min = K × |A| = 26 × 6 = 156 m → round up to 160 m (use even number for 20 m pegging)

Step 3 — Actual K-value

K = L / |A| = 160 / 6 = 26.67 (acceptable — exceeds minimum of 26)

Step 4 — Key Point Chainages and RLs

Chainage of PVC = PI − L/2 = 3200.000 − 80.000 = Ch. 3+120.000
Chainage of PVT = PI + L/2 = 3200.000 + 80.000 = Ch. 3+280.000

RL of PVC = RL_PVI − G1 × (L/2) / 100 = 65.450 − 4 × 80 / 100 = 65.450 − 3.200 = 62.250 m
RL of PVT = RL_PVI + G2 × (L/2) / 100 = 65.450 + (−2) × 80 / 100 = 65.450 − 1.600 = 63.850 m

Step 5 — Mid-curve Offset (e)

e = A × L / 800 = −6 × 160 / 800 = −1.200 m (negative = curve below PVI for crest ✓)

Step 6 — High Point Location

x_HP = −G1 × L / A = −(+4) × 160 / (−6) = 640 / 6 = 106.667 m from PVC
Chainage of High Point = 3120.000 + 106.667 = Ch. 3+226.667

Step 7 — Level Table at 20 m Intervals

Using RL_x = RL_PVC + G1·x/100 + (A/(200L))·x²

Parabola coefficient: A/(200L) = −6/(200×160) = −6/32000 = −0.0001875

Quick Check — PVT Level

Always verify the PVT level independently: RL_PVT = RL_PVI + G2 × (L/2)/100 = 65.450 + (−2)(80)/100 = 63.850 m. This matches the table — arithmetic is correct.

Worked Example 2 — Sag Curve

A valley on the same road (80 km/h) has G1 = −3% and G2 = +2.5%. PVI at chainage 5+460.000, RL 44.800 m. Design the curve.

Step 1 — Grade Difference

A = G2 − G1 = +2.5 − (−3) = +5.5% (positive → sag curve ✓)

Step 2 — Minimum Length

K_sag(min) at 80 km/h = 44
L_min = 44 × 5.5 = 242 m → use 260 m

Step 3 — Key Point Chainages and RLs

PVC chainage = 5460.000 − 130.000 = Ch. 5+330.000
PVT chainage = 5460.000 + 130.000 = Ch. 5+590.000
RL_PVC = 44.800 − (−3)(130)/100 = 44.800 + 3.900 = 48.700 m
RL_PVT = 44.800 + (2.5)(130)/100 = 44.800 + 3.250 = 48.050 m

Step 4 — Mid-curve Offset

e = +5.5 × 260 / 800 = +1.788 m (positive = curve above PVI for sag ✓)

Step 5 — Low Point Location

x_LP = −G1 × L / A = −(−3) × 260 / 5.5 = 780 / 5.5 = 141.818 m from PVC
Chainage of Low Point = 5330.000 + 141.818 = Ch. 5+471.818

The Low Point is Critical for Drainage

On a sag curve, the low point is where surface water naturally collects. The drainage engineer must ensure that a gully or inlet is located at or very near the low point chainage. Failure to drain at the low point causes ponding, aquaplaning and road damage. Always calculate and mark the low point chainage on your drawings.

Sight Distance Formulas

The sight distance design check is what produces the minimum K-values. Here are the exact formulas for both curve types, should you need to verify a sight distance for a non-standard situation:

Crest Curve — Stopping Sight Distance

Crest Curve SSD Check
When S ≤ L: L = A × S² / (100 × (√h1 + √h2)²) When S > L: L = 2S − (100 × (√h1 + √h2)²) / A
S = stopping sight distance (m) | h1 = driver eye height (typically 1.05 m) | h2 = object height (typically 0.26 m) | A in % | L in m. The simplified standard: K_crest = S² / 658 (using h1=1.05, h2=0.26).

Sag Curve — Headlight Sight Distance

Sag Curve Headlight SSD Check
When S ≤ L: L = A × S² / (100 × (C + S·tan β)) When S > L: L = 2S − 100(C + S·tan β) / A
C = headlight mounting height (typically 0.60 m) | tan β = beam angle (1° = 0.0175 rad) | Standard simplified: K_sag = S² / (120 + 3.5S). This is the formula behind the K values in the table above.

Summary — Design Procedure

01
Determine grades G1 and G2

From the longitudinal profile. Calculate A = G2 − G1. The sign of A tells you the curve type (negative = crest, positive = sag).

02
Look up minimum K for design speed and curve type

Use the K-value table (or national standard). Calculate minimum L = K × |A|. Round up to a convenient length (multiples of 20 m work well for pegging).

03
Calculate key point chainages and RLs

PVC chainage = PVI − L/2. PVT chainage = PVI + L/2. RL_PVC = RL_PVI − G1·(L/2)/100. RL_PVT = RL_PVI + G2·(L/2)/100.

04
Build the level table

At each peg position x from PVC: RL_x = RL_PVC + G1·x/100 + (A/200L)·x². Verify PVT level independently.

05
Calculate high/low point

x_HL = −G1·L/A from PVC. For crest: mark this on drawings for drainage. For sag: mark for inlet placement. Calculate its RL using the parabola equation.

Summary

Vertical curves are one of the core skills of road design. The parabola is used because it changes grade at a constant rate — giving the smoothest possible ride. The K-value system makes length selection simple: look up K for your design speed and curve type, multiply by the grade difference |A|, and you have your minimum curve length.

Remember the two key distinctions: crest curves are governed by daytime stopping sight distance (relatively low K values), while sag curves are governed by headlight sight distance at night (higher K values at high speeds). Always check both, always calculate the high/low point for drainage, and always verify your level table by independently calculating the PVT RL.
