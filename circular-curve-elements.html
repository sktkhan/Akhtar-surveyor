Circular Curve Design:
All Elements Explained

Whether you are designing a highway, setting out a road on site, or just studying for your engineering exam — here is everything you need to know about horizontal circular curves, written clearly by a road design engineer.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 11 min read Intermediate

Every road changes direction at some point. A perfectly straight road meeting another at a sharp corner is impossible to drive safely — so engineers design circular curves to connect two straight alignments with a smooth, controlled arc. The driver follows the curve comfortably, and the engineer sets it out precisely.

Understanding circular curves is fundamental for anyone working in road design, highway engineering or construction surveying. The geometry is based on a simple circle, but the terminology can be confusing at first. This article explains every element — what it is, why it matters, its formula, and how it fits into the bigger picture.

What This Article Covers

The full circular curve geometry — all six major elements (T, L, E, M, LC, D) with derivations — a complete worked example for R=200 m, Δ=40° — the deflection angle setting-out method — and practical field tips.

What is a Circular Curve?

A circular curve is a section of road alignment that follows a constant radius arc between two straight sections. The two straight sections are called tangents — because they are tangent to (just touching) the circle at the point where the curve begins and ends.

The point where the two tangents meet — if extended — is called the Point of Intersection (PI), also called the Intersection Point (IP). The angle they meet at is the intersection angle (I), and its supplement — the angle the curve turns through — is the delta angle (Δ), also called the deflection angle.

Delta (Δ) vs Intersection Angle (I)

If two tangents meet at an angle of 140°, the intersection angle I = 140°, and the delta (deflection) angle Δ = 180° − 140° = 40°. The delta angle is how much the road actually turns. Always use Δ in your curve calculations — not I.

Key Points on the Curve

Before learning the element formulas, you need to know the three key positions on every circular curve. Surveyors set these out in the field as the primary control points:

  • PC — Point of Curvature (also called TC — Tangent to Curve). Where the straight tangent ends and the curve begins. The first point you peg on site.
  • PT — Point of Tangency (also called CT — Curve to Tangent). Where the curve ends and the next straight tangent begins. The last point of the curve.
  • PI — Point of Intersection. Where the two tangents would meet if extended. Often inaccessible on site (in a field, over a cliff). The PI is a calculated position, not always physically marked.
  • O — Centre of Circle. The theoretical centre of the curve. Never physically accessible — it is a calculated geometric point used only in calculations.

The Six Curve Elements — Full Reference

Every circular curve is fully defined by just two values: the Radius (R) and the Delta angle (Δ). From these two, all six elements are calculated. Here they are — each one explained clearly:

T
Element 01
Tangent Length
T = R · tan(Δ/2)
The distance from the PC (or PT) back to the PI along the tangent line. This is what you subtract from the PI chainage to get the PC chainage, and add to get the PT chainage.
L
Element 02
Arc Length
L = R · Δ (radians)
L = π·R·Δ / 180
The actual curved distance from PC to PT along the arc. This is the length of road you are designing — it determines where the PT is in terms of chainage (PC chainage + L = PT chainage).
E
Element 03
External Distance
E = R · (sec(Δ/2) − 1)
The distance from the PI to the nearest point of the curve (the mid-arc point). Useful for checking sight distance clearance — any obstruction closer than E at the PI may block a driver's view around the curve.
M
Element 04
Middle Ordinate
M = R · (1 − cos(Δ/2))
The distance from the mid-point of the long chord to the mid-point of the arc. This is used in sight distance calculations — specifically for checking that vehicles on the inside of the curve can see ahead far enough to stop safely.
LC
Element 05
Long Chord
LC = 2 · R · sin(Δ/2)
The straight-line distance from PC to PT across the curve. This is a useful check — you can measure the LC in the field with a tape and verify that the PC and PT have been set out correctly.
D
Element 06
Degree of Curve
D = 1719.0 / R
The central angle subtended by a 30-metre arc (used in metric countries). Expresses "sharpness" of the curve. A large D means a tight, sharp curve. A small D means a gentle curve. Mainly used in railway and older highway design.
The Most Important Elements in Practice

T and L are the two you use on almost every job. T tells you where the PC and PT are in chainage. L tells you how long the curve is. E and M matter for sight distance checks. LC is your field verification tool. D is mainly for reference and railway work.

All Formulas — Quick Reference

Complete Circular Curve Formula Set
Tangent Length: T = R · tan(Δ/2) Arc Length: L = π · R · Δ / 180° External Dist.: E = R · (1/cos(Δ/2) − 1) Middle Ordinate: M = R · (1 − cos(Δ/2)) Long Chord: LC = 2 · R · sin(Δ/2) Degree of Curve: D = 1719.0 / R
Δ must be in degrees for the last formula and converted to radians (Δ × π/180) for the arc length formula.

Worked Example — R = 200 m, Δ = 40°

Let us work through a real example. A road alignment has two tangents meeting at a PI. The designer has chosen a radius of 200 metres, and the measured deflection angle is 40°00'00". Calculate all six curve elements and determine the chainage of PC and PT, given that the PI is at chainage 1+480.000.

Step 1 — Calculate Tangent Length (T)

T = R · tan(Δ/2) = 200 · tan(20°) = 200 × 0.36397 = 72.794 m

Step 2 — Calculate Arc Length (L)

L = π × 200 × 40 / 180 = 139.626 m

Step 3 — Calculate External Distance (E)

E = 200 × (1/cos(20°) − 1) = 200 × (1.06418 − 1) = 200 × 0.06418 = 12.835 m

Step 4 — Calculate Middle Ordinate (M)

M = 200 × (1 − cos(20°)) = 200 × (1 − 0.93969) = 200 × 0.06031 = 12.061 m

Step 5 — Calculate Long Chord (LC)

LC = 2 × 200 × sin(20°) = 400 × 0.34202 = 136.808 m

Step 6 — Calculate Degree of Curve (D)

D = 1719.0 / 200 = 8.595°

Step 7 — Calculate Chainages of PC and PT

Chainage of PC = PI − T = 1480.000 − 72.794 = Ch. 1+407.206
Chainage of PT = PC + L = 1407.206 + 139.626 = Ch. 1+546.832

Setting Out a Circular Curve in the Field

Calculating the elements is only half the job. The other half is actually getting those numbers onto the ground. There are several setting-out methods, but the two most common are the deflection angle method and the total station offset method.

Method 1 — Deflection Angle (Rankine's Method)

This is the classical method, used before total stations became common. The instrument is set up at the PC and the curve is set out point by point using deflection angles and chord lengths. It is still used today for small curves and as a check method.

The deflection angle to any point on the curve is exactly half the central angle to that point. For a chord of length c on a curve of radius R:

Deflection Angle Method
Deflection angle δ = (c / 2R) × (180/π) degrees For equal chords: δ = 1719.0 × c / R degrees (approx.)
Set up Total Station at PC, back-sight PI (set to 0°00'00"). Turn to each deflection angle and measure the chord distance to locate each curve point.

Final Check

The cumulative deflection at the PT must equal exactly Δ/2. In our example: 40°/2 = 20°00'00". If your table gives exactly this value, your setting-out data is correct. If not, find the arithmetic error before going to site.

Method 2 — Total Station Coordinates (Most Common Today)

The modern approach. Once you have the curve data, you calculate the E,N coordinates of every point along the curve using the geometry, then load them into your total station or data collector. The instrument sets out each point by coordinates — fast, accurate, and usable from any instrument position, not just the PC.

01
Calculate PC Coordinates

Using the PI coordinates, the bearing of the back tangent, and the tangent length T — calculate the E,N coordinates of the PC. This is a standard Bearing & Distance calculation.

02
Calculate Centre Coordinates

The centre O is perpendicular to the tangent at PC, at a distance R. Knowing the tangent bearing, rotate 90° (left or right depending on curve direction) and travel distance R to find O.

03
Compute Curve Points by Central Angle

For each point along the curve at arc distance s from PC, the central angle α = (s/R) × (180/π). The point coordinates are calculated as: E = Eo + R·sin(bearing to PC + α), N = No + R·cos(bearing to PC + α).

04
Set Out from Known Control

Load all coordinate points into the total station. Set up on any known control point with a clear view of the site, orient using a back-sight, then set out each curve point by coordinates — just stake each point as it comes up on screen.

How is Radius Chosen?

A common question from beginners: where does the radius come from? Who decides it? The answer is the design engineer — but it is not a free choice. The minimum radius for any curve depends on the design speed of the road and the amount of superelevation (road banking) that is acceptable.

The governing formula comes from vehicle dynamics — the relationship between speed, radius, superelevation and side friction:

Minimum Radius Formula
R_min = V² / (127 × (e_max + f_max))
V = design speed in km/h  |  e_max = maximum superelevation (e.g., 0.07 for 7%)  |  f_max = maximum side friction factor (depends on speed, typically 0.10–0.16)

For example, at a design speed of 100 km/h with e=0.07 and f=0.12: R_min = 10000 / (127 × 0.19) = 415 m. No curve on this road may have a radius less than 415 m. In practice, designers use standard tables from national design manuals (AASHTO, UK Design Manual for Roads, etc.) rather than calculating this each time.

Conclusion

Circular curves are at the heart of every road and railway alignment. Understanding the six elements — T, L, E, M, LC and D — is not just academic knowledge. These are the numbers that get pegged into the ground, checked with a tape, and verified by the resident engineer before any construction proceeds.

The two you will use most often are T (to locate the PC and PT by chainage) and L (to determine the curve length and PT chainage). The rest matter for sight distance design, field checks and reporting. Learn them all, and use the calculator to verify your hand calculations.
