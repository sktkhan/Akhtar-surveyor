Reduced Level (RL) Calculation:
The Height of Instrument Method

If you have ever stood behind a level instrument wondering what those numbers in the staff book actually mean — this guide is for you. We break it all down: what an RL is, how to calculate it, and how to check your work.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 10 min read Intermediate

Levelling is one of the most fundamental operations in surveying. You do it before you design a road, lay a drainage channel, set out a building, or plan an irrigation scheme. And at the heart of levelling is one simple concept: the Reduced Level.

Once you understand what a Reduced Level is and how to calculate it, a whole world of engineering surveying opens up. The math is not complicated — it is mostly addition and subtraction. But the logic needs to be clear, and the arithmetic checks need to be correct. That is what this article is for.

What You Will Learn

What a Reduced Level (RL) is — the Height of Instrument (HI) method step by step — a fully worked booking table — the arithmetic check — comparison with the Rise & Fall method — and the most common mistakes beginners make.

What is a Reduced Level?

A Reduced Level (RL) is the height of a point above a fixed reference datum — usually Mean Sea Level (MSL). Think of it as the elevation of a point. If a point has an RL of 52.340 m, it means that point is 52.340 metres above mean sea level.

The word "reduced" simply means the level has been reduced to the datum — we have calculated it relative to a common reference so all our points are comparable. It does not mean the number is small or has been decreased.

📏
Datum (Mean Sea Level) RL = 0.000 m
───────────────────────────────
Ground Floor, Building A RL = 48.230 m
Road Centreline, Ch. 0+00 RL = 49.560 m
Top of Kerb RL = 49.920 m
Top of Wall RL = 52.340 m
Fig 1. All points are described by their RL — their height above the same datum. This makes them directly comparable.

In practice, most surveys do not go back to mean sea level directly. Instead, they start from a Bench Mark (BM) — a fixed point with a known, officially recorded RL. The surveyor starts their level run from this bench mark and works outward, calculating the RL of every other point along the way.

Key Terms You Must Know

Before we get into the calculation, make sure you understand these five terms. They appear in every level book and every RL calculation you will ever do:

  • Bench Mark (BM) — A fixed reference point with a known, established RL. Your starting point for the level run.
  • Back Sight (BS) — The first staff reading taken at each instrument setup. Always taken on a known point (the BM or a change point). Used to calculate the Height of Instrument.
  • Intermediate Sight (IS) — Any staff reading taken between a BS and FS, on points you need the RL of. These are your "working" readings.
  • Fore Sight (FS) — The last staff reading at each instrument setup. Taken before you move the instrument. Ends the instrument position.
  • Height of Instrument (HI) — The elevation of the line of sight of the instrument. Calculated as: BM RL + BS reading.
Memory Aid — BS, IS, FS

Think of it this way: BS = "I just set up, let me look back at something I know." IS = "I'm looking at points I need." FS = "I'm about to move — last reading before I go." Each instrument position has exactly one BS, one FS, and any number of IS readings.

The Levelling Instrument

Levelling is done using a level instrument — which gives you a perfectly horizontal line of sight — combined with a levelling staff, which is a graduated rod that the chainman holds vertically at each point to be measured.

The Height of Instrument (HI) Method — Explained

There are two standard methods of booking and calculating reduced levels: the Height of Instrument (HI) method and the Rise and Fall method. The HI method is faster for large numbers of IS readings, and it is the one most used in construction surveys. Let us go through it step by step.

The Core Logic

The idea is elegant. If you know the RL of a point (the BM) and you know how high the staff reading is at that point (the BS), you can calculate how high your instrument's line of sight is above datum. That is the Height of Instrument (HI).

Once you have the HI, any other staff reading subtracted from it gives you the RL of that point — because the staff is sitting on the ground, the instrument is looking horizontally at the staff, and the difference tells you where the ground is relative to your datum.

The Two Core Formulas
Height of Instrument (HI) = RL of BM + Back Sight (BS) Reduced Level (RL) = HI − Staff Reading (IS or FS)
These two lines are the entire HI method. Everything else is just applying them repeatedly at each instrument setup.
"Add the BS to the BM to get up to the instrument. Subtract any reading from the HI to come back down to the ground."

Worked Example — Complete Level Run

Let us work through a real example. We have a level run with two instrument setups, a bench mark, four intermediate staff positions, and we close back onto a Temporary Bench Mark (TBM).

Here is the raw field data as collected in the level book:

  • BM RL = 50.000 m (known, from official survey)
  • Setup 1 → BS on BM = 1.525, IS on P1 = 1.210, IS on P2 = 0.840, FS on CP1 = 1.675
  • Setup 2 → BS on CP1 = 1.380, IS on P3 = 1.055, IS on P4 = 1.620, FS on TBM = 0.935

Step 1: Calculate HI for Setup 1

HI₁ = RL of BM + BS = 50.000 + 1.525 = 51.525 m

Step 2: Calculate RLs for Setup 1

RL of P1 = HI₁ − IS = 51.525 − 1.210 = 50.315 m
RL of P2 = HI₁ − IS = 51.525 − 0.840 = 50.685 m
RL of CP1 = HI₁ − FS = 51.525 − 1.675 = 49.850 m

Step 3: Calculate HI for Setup 2

HI₂ = RL of CP1 + BS = 49.850 + 1.380 = 51.230 m

Step 4: Calculate RLs for Setup 2

RL of P3 = HI₂ − IS = 51.230 − 1.055 = 50.175 m
RL of P4 = HI₂ − IS = 51.230 − 1.620 = 49.610 m
RL of TBM = HI₂ − FS = 51.230 − 0.935 = 50.295 m

The Completed Booking Table

Here is how all of this looks in a professional level book — the format used on every construction site and in every surveying office:

The Arithmetic Check — Never Skip This

The HI method has a built-in arithmetic check. After you finish the booking, you must run this check before declaring your level run complete. If it does not pass, you have made an arithmetic error somewhere and need to find it.

Arithmetic Check — HI Method
Sum of BS − Sum of FS = Last RL − First RL
The difference of all backsights minus all foresights must equal the difference between the last and first reduced levels. If it does, your arithmetic is correct.

Let us verify our worked example:

Check ItemCalculationResultStatus
Sum of all BS 1.525 + 1.380 2.905 —
Sum of all FS 1.675 + 0.935 2.610 —
ΣBS − ΣFS 2.905 − 2.610 +0.295 —
Last RL − First RL 50.295 − 50.000 +0.295 —
Do they match? 0.295 = 0.295 ✓ YES PASS ✓
Important Limitation of the HI Method Check

The arithmetic check only verifies that your addition and subtraction are correct. It does not check the accuracy of your field readings. A misread staff reading (e.g., reading 1.215 instead of 1.515) will pass the check but give wrong RLs. This is why the Rise & Fall method, which checks every IS reading individually, is preferred for precise work.

HI Method vs Rise & Fall Method

The HI method is fast and suits construction surveys where you have many IS readings and need quick results. The Rise and Fall (R&F) method is slower but checks every single intermediate sight individually. Here is a quick comparison:

FeatureHI MethodRise & Fall Method
Speed⚡ Faster🐢 Slower
Arithmetic CheckChecks BS & FS onlyChecks every reading
IS reading errorsNot detected by checkDetected by check
Best forConstruction, many ISPrecise geodetic levelling
ComplexitySimple — 2 formulasMore steps per reading
Industry usageVery commonLess common

For most construction and engineering surveys, the HI method is the standard. If you are doing a primary control level run for a large project or government work, use Rise and Fall — the more thorough check is worth the extra time.

Understanding Change Points

You cannot always see your entire level run from one instrument position. Mountains, buildings, or distance make it necessary to move the instrument during a run. A Change Point (CP) is how you do this without losing your datum.

01
Take the last FS reading

At your current setup, take the final staff reading (FS) on a solid, stable point — a concrete corner, a peg hammered firmly in, a step. This point becomes CP1. Record the FS and calculate the RL of CP1.

02
Move the instrument

Pick up the tripod and instrument and move to the new position. The staff man stays at CP1 — do not move the staff. This is critical: the staff must not move between the FS and BS readings at a change point.

03
Take the first BS reading at new position

From the new instrument position, take a BS on CP1 (the staff has not moved). Calculate the new HI: HI₂ = RL of CP1 + new BS. Continue your run from here.

Never Do This at a Change Point

Do not move the staff while you are moving the instrument. If the staff moves even a few millimetres, your new HI will be calculated from the wrong RL and every subsequent reading will be in error — and the arithmetic check will not catch it.

Common Mistakes — And How to Avoid Them

In over a decade of levelling, these are the mistakes that appear most often — especially from students and less experienced engineers:

❌ Wrong

Moving the staff during a change point — invalidating the link between your two instrument setups.

✓ Correct

Staff stays absolutely still at the change point while the instrument is moved. Only the instrument moves.

❌ Wrong

Reading the staff upside down — misreading 1.215 as 1.512. A common error with older graduated staves.

✓ Correct

Read the staff carefully — numbers increase upward. Call out your reading aloud for the booker to confirm.

❌ Wrong

Booking a BS reading as an IS, or confusing which column to enter readings into in the level book.

✓ Correct

First reading of each setup = BS. Last reading before moving = FS. Anything in between = IS. Simple.

❌ Wrong

Skipping the arithmetic check. Always check — if it fails, find and fix the error before leaving the office.

✓ Correct

Run the check every time: ΣBS − ΣFS = Last RL − First RL. If it passes, your arithmetic is confirmed.

Closing Error and Adjustment

In professional surveying, a level run is almost always run as a closed loop — you start from a known BM, survey your points, and then level back to either the same BM or another BM with a known RL. This allows you to check how accurate your field work was.

The closing error is the difference between the calculated RL at the closing BM and its known RL. If your calculated RL matches the known RL exactly, your error is zero — practically impossible. In reality, there will always be a small discrepancy.

Closing Error and Acceptable Accuracy
Closing Error (e) = Calculated RL − Known RL Acceptable Error = ±12√K mm (where K = distance in km)
For example, over a 2 km level run: acceptable error = ±12√2 = ±17 mm. If your closing error exceeds this, the run must be repeated.

If the closing error is within the acceptable limit, it is distributed proportionally across all the intermediate points — a process called adjustment. Points measured over longer distances from the BM receive a slightly larger correction.

Summary

The Height of Instrument method is a practical, efficient way to calculate reduced levels across any site. Once you understand the logic — add the BS to get up to the HI, subtract any reading to come back down to the ground — it becomes second nature.

The key habits that separate a good surveyor from a careless one are simple: always run the arithmetic check, never move the staff at a change point, read carefully, and close your loop on a known BM whenever possible.
