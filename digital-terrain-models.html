Digital Terrain Models:
How Contours and Volumes Are Calculated

A Digital Terrain Model turns a scattered cloud of survey points into a mathematical surface that can generate contours, calculate earthwork volumes and answer questions about any point on the ground. Understanding how this works makes you a better surveyor and a better earthworks engineer.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 13 min read Earthworks

Before a single cubic metre of soil is moved on a large earthworks project, the engineer needs to know how much cut and fill is involved. Before a drainage design can be produced, the engineer needs contours showing how water will flow. Before a road alignment can be optimised, the designer needs a continuous terrain surface to work against. All of these require a Digital Terrain Model — or DTM.

The DTM is one of the most powerful outputs of a topographic survey. It is not a picture, not a drawing, and not a point cloud. It is a mathematical surface that can be queried at any location, used in volume calculations, and rendered into any contour interval the engineer needs. This article explains how a DTM is built from survey data, how contours are generated, and how volumes are calculated from it.

What This Article Covers

What a DTM is and how it differs from a DSM — the survey-to-DTM pipeline — TIN triangulation and the Delaunay algorithm — how contours are interpolated — three volume calculation methods (grid, prismoidal, TIN) — a worked cut-and-fill example — practical data collection tips — and common mistakes.

What Exactly is a DTM?

The term Digital Terrain Model (DTM) refers to a digital representation of the bare-earth surface — the ground, without trees, buildings or other surface features. A closely related term is Digital Surface Model (DSM), which includes everything above the ground: buildings, trees, bridges. The DTM strips all of that away to show only the terrain.

  • DTM — Bare earth surface. Represents the ground. Used for earthworks, drainage design, flood modelling, road design
  • DSM — All surfaces including buildings and vegetation. Used for line-of-sight analysis, urban planning, photogrammetry outputs
  • DEM — Digital Elevation Model. Often used interchangeably with DTM, but technically a broader term that includes both DSM and DTM
  • TIN — Triangulated Irregular Network. The mathematical structure most commonly used to represent a DTM in engineering software

In construction surveying, when engineers say "the DTM," they almost always mean a TIN surface built from total station or GPS survey points, representing the bare ground.

"The DTM is the digital twin of the ground — once you have it, you can answer any terrain question without going back to the field."

TIN Triangulation — How the Surface is Built

The most common mathematical structure for representing a DTM in engineering software is the Triangulated Irregular Network (TIN). A TIN connects survey points with triangular faces to form a continuous surface. The key properties of a TIN are:

  • Every survey point is an exact vertex — the TIN passes through every measured point precisely
  • The surface between points is defined by the flat triangular face — it is a linear interpolation within each triangle
  • No point in the survey area is further than one triangle away from a measured value
  • The triangle sizes adapt to the density of survey data — closely spaced points in detailed areas, larger triangles where the terrain is smooth and fewer shots were taken

The Delaunay Triangulation Algorithm

Given a set of points, there are many ways to connect them into triangles. The Delaunay triangulation is the one that engineering software almost universally uses, because it produces the "best" triangles in a specific geometric sense: it maximises the minimum angle of all triangles, which means it avoids very long, thin slivers that interpolate poorly.

The mathematical property that defines Delaunay triangulation is the circumcircle criterion: for any triangle in the network, the circumscribed circle (passing through all three vertices) contains no other survey point in its interior. If you find a quadrilateral (two adjacent triangles) that violates this rule, you "flip" the shared edge to create two new triangles that satisfy it. This process repeats until all triangles satisfy the criterion.

Contour Generation — How It Works

Once the TIN is built, generating contours is a geometric interpolation problem. For each triangle in the TIN, the software tests whether a given contour elevation (say, 43.000 m) passes through that triangle. If it does, the exact positions where the contour crosses the triangle edges are computed by linear interpolation:

Contour Intersection — Linear Interpolation on a TIN Edge
Position along edge = (H_contour − H_start) / (H_end − H_start) E_intersection = E_start + t × (E_end − E_start) N_intersection = N_start + t × (N_end − N_start)
Where t = position along edge (0 to 1), H_contour = desired contour elevation, H_start and H_end = heights at each end of the TIN edge. Each triangle that is crossed by the contour contributes a short line segment. All segments are joined to form the complete contour line. This is done for every triangle and every contour elevation in sequence.

This process is exact — the contours produced from a TIN are mathematically precise linear interpolations between measured survey points. The accuracy of the contours depends entirely on the density and accuracy of the original survey data, not on the contouring software.

Contour Interval — Choosing the Right Value

The contour interval should be chosen based on the point density of the survey and the intended use. As a guide: if your average shot spacing is D metres, the minimum meaningful contour interval is approximately D × tan(slope angle). For a 10 m shot spacing on a 5% slope, the minimum useful interval is about 0.5 m. Generating 0.1 m contours from 20 m spaced shots creates false precision — the interpolation between points will not represent the real terrain at that resolution.

Volume Calculation — Three Methods

Comparing an existing ground DTM with a design surface DTM gives you the cut-and-fill volumes for an earthworks project. There are three main methods:

Grid Method

Average End Area / Grid Squares

The site is divided into a regular grid. At each grid node, the height difference (existing − design) is read from both DTMs. The volume of each grid cell is approximated as the average height difference multiplied by the cell area.

Simple and fast. Accuracy improves with finer grid. Does not follow irregular boundaries well.

Best for: Large-scale earthworks, quick estimates
Prismoidal Formula

Simpson's Rule Refinement

A correction to the average end area method that accounts for the curved nature of the surface. The prismoidal formula adds a mid-section area term that improves accuracy, particularly for curved sections.

More accurate than simple averaging. Requires additional mid-point measurements or interpolation.

Best for: Road sections, linear earthworks
TIN vs TIN

Surface Comparison

Both the existing ground and the design surface are represented as TINs. The two surfaces are overlaid and the volume of the solid between them is computed exactly for each triangular prism element. This is the most accurate method.

Exact to the precision of the survey data. Handles complex boundaries and shapes.

Best for: All precision earthworks, final quantities
Grid Volume Method — Formula
h_i = H_existing(i) − H_design(i) [at each grid node i] V_cell = Cell Area × Average h of four corners V_cell = (D² / 4) × (h₁ + h₂ + h₃ + h₄) Total cut volume = Σ V_cell for all cells where h > 0 Total fill volume = Σ V_cell for all cells where h < 0
D = grid spacing (m). h = height difference at each corner (positive = cut, negative = fill). Grid cells that straddle the zero line (some corners cut, some fill) require subdivision or interpolation of the boundary. All volume methods report gross cut and gross fill separately — the net difference is the bulk earthworks balance.
Prismoidal Formula (for linear sections)
V = (L / 6) × (A₁ + 4A_m + A₂)
L = length of section (m) | A₁ = cross-sectional area at start | A₂ = cross-sectional area at end | A_m = cross-sectional area at mid-point. This is Simpson's rule applied to volume — it gives exact results for prismoidal shapes (pyramids, wedges, frusta) and a very good approximation for real earthworks cross sections.

Bulking and Shrinkage Factors

The volumes above are in-situ volumes. When soil is excavated it bulks (expands) — typically 15–25% depending on soil type. When placed and compacted it shrinks — typically 5–15% below the in-situ volume. Always apply bulking/shrinkage factors before ordering plant or haulage. A 1000 m³ in-situ cut produces approximately 1200 m³ of loose material to haul, which compacts to approximately 900 m³ in the fill area.

Practical Tips for Good DTM Data Collection

🎯
Shoot Terrain Changes, Not a Grid

The most important survey shots are at points where the terrain changes direction — ridgelines, valley floors, break-of-slope, toe of embankment. A regular grid misses these and the TIN interpolates wrongly across them. Always shoot break lines first, then fill with regular shots.

📐
Define Break Lines Explicitly

In your survey software, mark important linear features (roads, ditches, walls, embankment toes) as break lines. The TIN will honour these edges exactly — triangles will not cross a break line. Without break lines, the TIN interpolates across sharp changes and contours become misleading.

📏
Match Shot Density to Required Contour Interval

If you need 0.5 m contours, you need shots close enough to define the terrain at that resolution. On a 5% slope, 0.5 m contours are 10 m apart on the ground — so shots every 5–8 m are needed. On a 20% slope, 0.5 m contours are 2.5 m apart — shots every 1–2 m are needed in that area.

🚧
Extend the Survey Beyond the Site Boundary

Always survey at least 5–10 m beyond the project boundary. The TIN needs data outside the design area to correctly define the surface at the edges — otherwise boundary effects distort the contours and volumes near the site perimeter.

💧
Always Capture Drainage Features

Survey the invert (bottom) of all channels, drains, culverts and gullies. These are break lines in the terrain that the TIN must honour. If a drain is not captured accurately, the DTM will show water flowing in the wrong direction — which invalidates the entire drainage design.

🔍
Check the TIN Visually Before Using It

Always render the TIN and contours on screen before producing any output. Look for: spiky anomalies (bad survey shots), flat areas (insufficient data), contours crossing (gross errors), or implausible drainage patterns. Fix problems in the source data — do not smooth or delete TIN triangles to hide survey errors.

Common DTM Mistakes

  • Missing break lines: The TIN interpolates across a road cutting or embankment edge, producing wrong contours and volumes. Always add break lines along all significant terrain discontinuities
  • Boundary effect errors: The TIN is not extended beyond the site, so edge triangles are very large and volumes near the boundary are wildly wrong. Survey beyond the site
  • Gross error shots: One mistyped height (e.g. 45.200 recorded as 4.520) creates a spike in the TIN that distorts all surrounding contours and volumes. Inspect the raw data before building the TIN
  • Wrong coordinate system: Design DTM is in site grid; survey DTM is in national coordinates; volumes are computed from the difference — meaningless. Always confirm coordinate systems match before any comparison
  • Ignoring bulking/shrinkage: Reporting in-situ volumes as if they are fill volumes leads to ordering too little material or too little haulage capacity. Always apply soil factors for earthworks quantities
  • Too coarse a grid for the method: Using 20 m grid squares on a site with significant terrain variation — the averaging misses peaks and valleys, underestimating both cut and fill. Use a finer grid or switch to TIN vs TIN comparison

Summary

A Digital Terrain Model is not magic — it is a systematic mathematical process. Survey points are collected at the terrain changes that matter most. The Delaunay triangulation algorithm connects those points into a TIN surface that passes exactly through every measured point and interpolates linearly between them. Contours are generated by intersecting horizontal planes with the TIN edges. Volumes are calculated by comparing two TIN surfaces — existing ground and design — and summing the prismatic solid between them.

The quality of a DTM depends almost entirely on the quality of the input survey data. More points in the right places (at terrain changes, along break lines, at drainage features) produce a better DTM than twice as many points on a regular grid in flat areas. And the output — whether contours, volumes or design check surfaces — is only as reliable as the person who set up the TIN correctly and inspected it visually before trusting any numbers.

Understanding the DTM pipeline — from field shot to TIN to contour to volume — makes you a better commissioner of surveys, a better checker of software output, and a better engineer when the earthworks quantities matter to the project budget.
