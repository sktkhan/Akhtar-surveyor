Total Station Resection:
Free Station Setup Explained

You arrive at a site, you cannot set up on any of your control points, and you need to start working immediately. The free station resection is how professional surveyors solve this problem every day — and it is one of the most powerful techniques in practical field surveying.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 11 min read Field Practice

On a typical construction site, your total station control points are pegs and bolts driven into the ground — often in positions chosen months ago for traverse purposes, not for daily convenience. On a given day, you may find a peg has been disturbed, is behind a temporary fence, buried under fill, or simply in an inconvenient position that requires carrying equipment up a slope. The question is: can you still work, and work accurately?

The answer is yes — using resection, also known as free station setup. Instead of setting up on a known point and aiming at known reference directions, you set up anywhere convenient and observe known control points. The total station (or you, manually) calculates where the instrument is located. Once you know the instrument position, you can set out or survey normally.

What This Article Covers

Classic backsight setup vs free station — when to use resection — the two-point method — the three-point method for redundancy — the geometry rules that determine resection quality — the calculation process — the dangerous circle problem — common field mistakes — and practical tips from real site experience.

Classic Setup vs Free Station — A Direct Comparison

Classic Backsight Setup

Set Up on a Known Point

The total station is centred and levelled exactly over a known control point (a peg or bolt with known E,N coordinates). You then aim at a second known point to establish your orientation (the backsight). All subsequent measurements are referenced to this orientation.

  • Simple and fast when you can access the control point
  • Requires precise centring over the peg
  • Limited to positions that have known control
  • If the peg is disturbed, you have a serious problem
Free Station (Resection)

Set Up Anywhere

The total station is set up at any convenient position — on stable ground, with a clear view of at least two (preferably three) known control points. You measure angles and distances to those known points, and the software calculates where the instrument is.

  • Setup position is totally flexible — go where the work is
  • No centring over a peg required
  • Requires visibility to 2+ known control points
  • Three points gives a redundancy check on quality
"Resection does not just give you flexibility — it is often more accurate than a classic setup, because you can choose the instrument position that gives the best line of sight to the work area."

When to Use Resection

Resection is the right choice in many situations that are common on active construction sites:

  • Your nearest control peg has been disturbed, buried or removed by site works
  • The control points are at the wrong end of the site — setting out requires long distances with poor accuracy
  • You need to set up inside a structure (a building frame, an underground excavation) where no existing control is located
  • You need a high instrument position (on scaffolding, on top of a wall) for line of sight reasons
  • You want to work quickly without setting up a tripod precisely over a peg in rough terrain
  • You need to monitor a structure from a stable position that is not a survey peg
Resection is the Standard on Modern Sites

Most modern total station work on engineering construction sites uses resection as the primary setup method. The classic backsight-from-a-peg method is still used for traverse work and control establishment — but for day-to-day setting out, free station is faster, more flexible, and often more reliable.

The Geometry — How the Calculation Works

The mathematics of resection is elegant. Given the coordinates of two or three known points and the measured horizontal angles between them (as seen from the unknown instrument station), you can solve for the position of the instrument. Modern total stations do this calculation internally — but understanding the geometry helps you choose good setup positions and recognise when results are unreliable.

Two-Point Resection

With two known points, you measure the horizontal angle between them and the distance to each (using EDM). From these, you get two distance equations and the geometry is solvable — one unique solution exists as long as the geometry is not degenerate (see the dangerous circle, below).

Two-Point Resection — Core Relations
Distance to A: DA = measured EDM distance to control point A Distance to B: DB = measured EDM distance to control point B E_P = E_A + DA × sin(bearing A→P) [from each known point] N_P = N_A + DA × cos(bearing A→P)
The total station computes the bearing from A to P using the measured angle and the known bearing A→B. The two equations from points A and B are solved simultaneously. In practice the total station does this internally — you just observe the points and read the result.

Three-Point Resection — With Redundancy

With three known points, you have three observations — more than the minimum needed (two). This gives you a redundant observation, which means you can detect and quantify errors. The total station uses a least-squares solution to find the best-fit instrument position, and it can report a residual (the mismatch between the computed positions from each pair of points). If the residual is small, confidence is high. If it is large, something is wrong — the wrong coordinates, a wrong prism position, or poor geometry.

Fig 1. Free station resection geometry. The total station at P observes three known control points A, B and C. From the measured distances DA, DB, DC and the horizontal angles between them, P's coordinates are computed. Three points gives redundancy and a quality check.

The Dangerous Circle — Critical Geometry Warning

There is one geometric configuration that makes a resection unreliable or even impossible: when the instrument station and all observed control points lie on the same circle. This is called the dangerous circle (or degenerate case).

When this happens, there are infinitely many positions on the circle that would produce the same observed angles — the solution becomes non-unique and the total station software may give a wrong result, or a large residual, or refuse to solve. Many total stations warn of this automatically — but not all do, and the warning may be easy to miss.

Configurations to Avoid

⚠️ All Points Collinear

If all known points and the instrument are approximately on a straight line, the geometry is very poor. Small errors cause large position errors.

⚠️ Very Small Angles

If the angle subtended at the instrument between two control points is less than 15–20°, the geometry is weak. Observe a third, more widely spaced point.

⚠️ P on the Dangerous Circle

If the instrument, both control points, and the circle through them are co-circular, the solution is singular. A third point outside the circle resolves this.

⚠️ Residual Too Large

A three-point residual over 5–10 mm (for engineering work) indicates poor observations, wrong coordinates, or poor geometry. Do not accept the result.

Geometry Rules for a Good Resection

✓ 30°–150° Angle Spread

Angles between control points as seen from the instrument should be between 30° and 150°. This gives the most stable solution geometry.

✓ Use Three Points

Always use three known points when possible. The redundant observation detects errors and gives you a quantified quality indicator (residual).

✓ Points Around the Station

Ideally, control points should surround the instrument position rather than all being in one direction. Spread of at least 120° is ideal.

✓ Check Residual < 5 mm

For engineering setting out, accept a three-point resection only if the position residual is within 5 mm. Repeat the setup if it exceeds this.

Field Procedure — Step by Step

Here is the exact procedure for a three-point free station resection with a modern total station. The steps are the same regardless of instrument brand — the menus differ, but the sequence is identical.

01
Choose your instrument position

Pick a stable position on firm ground with clear lines of sight to at least three control points. Check the geometry mentally — are the known points spread around you? Are angles between them at least 30°? Avoid positions on or near the dangerous circle.

02
Set up and level the instrument

Tripod on firm ground, spread legs wide for stability. Rough level with the tripod legs, fine level with the instrument foot screws. The instrument does not need to be centred over any peg — just stable and level. Check level bubble after each tripod leg adjustment.

03
Enter the known control point coordinates

In the total station's resection menu, enter the E,N (and H if doing 3D) coordinates of each control point you plan to observe. Double-check each entry — a coordinate entry error is the single most common cause of a bad resection.

04
Aim and measure to the first control point

Centre the crosshairs precisely on the prism at control point A. Take the EDM distance measurement. The total station records the horizontal angle and distance. Repeat in face left and face right if high accuracy is needed.

05
Observe the second and third control points

Without disturbing the instrument, aim at prisms at B and C in sequence. Take EDM distances at each. The total station now has enough data to compute the solution.

06
Accept the solution — check the residual

The instrument displays the computed E,N coordinates of the free station and the residual (the disagreement between the solution from each pair of points). If residual is within your tolerance (typically 5–10 mm for construction), accept. If not, re-observe or add a fourth control point.

07
Do a final check observation

Before starting work, aim at one of your control points and read its displayed distance and bearing. Independently calculate what those values should be from the computed station coordinates. If they agree within 5 mm and 5 arc seconds, you are good to proceed.

What the Instrument Calculates

Most engineers using resection let the total station handle the calculation. But understanding the method behind it helps you troubleshoot and verify results. The two most common mathematical approaches are:

Collins Point Method (Two-Point Resection)

A classic analytical method used for hand calculation and historical instruments. It uses the angles at the instrument station to construct a helper point (the Collins Point) on the circumscribed circle of the three points, then solves the triangle. It is elegant and exact but requires careful arithmetic — best handled by a calculator or spreadsheet.

Least Squares (Three or More Points)

Modern total stations use a least-squares iterative solution for three or more control points. Starting from an approximate station position, the algorithm adjusts the E,N coordinates until the residuals between observed and computed angles and distances are minimised. This is fast (typically converging in 3–5 iterations) and produces both the best-fit position and a quality indicator (residual).

The Core Resection Check
Computed bearing P→A = atan2(E_A − E_P, N_A − N_P) Computed distance P→A = √((E_A−E_P)² + (N_A−N_P)²) Residual = observed value − computed value (should be near zero)
After the instrument computes E_P and N_P, it recalculates the expected bearing and distance to each control point and compares with the observed values. The difference is the residual. For a two-point resection, residual is zero by definition — this is why three points (with redundancy) is always preferred.

Common Mistakes — And How to Avoid Them

❌ Wrong

Entering coordinates in the wrong order — transposing Easting and Northing, or using the wrong control point number. This is the most frequent cause of a completely wrong resection result.

✓ Correct

Read coordinates aloud from the control point schedule, have another person check the entry, and always verify the coordinate entry by re-reading from the instrument screen before observing.

❌ Wrong

Accepting a large residual because you are in a hurry. A 25 mm residual on a 200 m setup means your setting-out could be off by 25 mm or more — unacceptable on structural or precise engineering work.

✓ Correct

Define your maximum acceptable residual before going to site (5 mm for precise work, 10 mm for earthworks). If the result exceeds it, repeat the setup or use a different station position.

❌ Wrong

Not checking the instrument height (HI) when doing 3D resection. The HI affects all reduced levels computed from the free station — a 10 mm HI error propagates to every staked level.

✓ Correct

Measure instrument height twice, from opposite sides of the instrument, with a steel tape to the survey mark on the tripod head. Average both measurements and enter carefully.

❌ Wrong

Using only two control points with poor geometry — both far away in the same direction, giving a very small angle between them at the instrument. The resulting position can be metres wrong.

✓ Correct

Always use three control points. If only two are visible, ensure the angle between them at the instrument is at least 30° — and verify the result with an independent check observation to a third known point.

Always Verify Your Free Station

No matter how good your residual looks, always do an independent verification before starting setting-out work. The most reliable verification is to set out a known coordinate point that you did not use in the resection — then physically check whether the prism lands on the existing peg.

  1. After the resection is accepted, choose a known control point you did not observe during the setup
  2. Set out that point by coordinates from the free station
  3. Check whether the instrument's calculated direction and distance match where the actual peg is
  4. If the discrepancy is within your tolerance, proceed with confidence
  5. If not — repeat the resection, check coordinates, and investigate before any setting-out work begins
Never Trust a Single Unverified Resection

A resection that uses only the points you observed in its own solution cannot detect errors in those reference coordinates, or a prism on the wrong peg, or a coordinate entry error. The independent check using a point outside the resection solution is what catches these problems. This step takes two minutes and can save days of rework.

Resection vs GNSS — When Each Is Better

GNSS (GPS/GLONASS/Galileo) receivers can also give you a position anywhere on site without setting up on a known peg. So why use resection at all? The answer is precision, reliability in obstructed areas, and traceability to the site control network:

  • Resection accuracy: 2–5 mm with a good total station in good conditions. GNSS RTK: typically 10–20 mm horizontal, 20–30 mm vertical
  • Obstructed skies: Inside buildings, under bridges, near walls — GNSS fails completely. Resection with a total station works perfectly
  • Network traceability: Resection uses the same site control network as all other survey work — everything is in the same coordinate system without transformation. GNSS requires a site calibration to relate GPS to site coordinates
  • Vertical precision: GNSS height accuracy is typically 2–3× worse than horizontal. For precise level setting, total station resection with a good height measurement is superior

Summary

The free station resection is one of the most practical and powerful techniques in modern field surveying. It gives you total flexibility over where you set up, removes the requirement to occupy specific pegs, and — when done correctly with three known points — provides a built-in quality check through the residual.

The keys to a reliable resection are: good geometry (angles spread, not all in one direction), three control points for redundancy, careful coordinate entry, a residual check within your project tolerance, and an independent verification observation before starting any work.

Avoid the dangerous circle, always check your residual, always verify with a point outside your resection, and always measure instrument height carefully when levels are involved. Follow these rules and the free station setup will serve you reliably on every site you work on.
