Traverse Calculations:
Bowditch Adjustment from Start to Finish

A traverse is one of the most powerful tools in a surveyor's toolkit — a chain of measured angles and distances that builds a coordinate framework across a site. This article walks you through the complete calculation sequence from raw field angles to final adjusted coordinates.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 14 min read Intermediate

Every engineering project needs a coordinate framework — a set of control points with known positions from which all other measurements and setting-out work can be referenced. For most sites, this framework is established by a traverse: a series of survey stations connected by measured horizontal angles and distances.

The calculation that follows the fieldwork — converting raw angles and distances into adjusted coordinates — is one of the most satisfying in surveying once you understand the logic. You measure, you compute, you find the error, and then you distribute it systematically. This article explains exactly how.

What This Article Covers

Types of traverses — key terminology — the complete 7-step calculation sequence — bearing computation from interior angles — latitudes and departures — closing error and accuracy ratio — the Bowditch (Compass Rule) adjustment — final coordinate calculation — and a complete 4-station worked example.

What is a Traverse?

A traverse is a connected series of survey lines, where each line has a measured horizontal distance and the angle between consecutive lines has been measured. From a known starting point and a known starting bearing, you can calculate the position of every subsequent point along the traverse.

🔁
Closed Loop Traverse

Starts and ends at the same point (or two known points). The closure allows you to calculate the error and assess accuracy. This is the standard type used for control surveys, property surveys, and most engineering work.

➡️
Open (Link) Traverse

Starts at one known point and finishes at a different known point. Can also be closed on bearing only. Less common — used for corridor surveys (pipelines, roads) where a loop is not practical.

For this article, we focus entirely on the closed loop traverse — the most common type in engineering surveying and the one where the Bowditch adjustment applies.

Key Terminology

  • Station — A survey point in the traverse. Usually labelled A, B, C… or 1, 2, 3…
  • Leg — A line between two consecutive stations, with a measured distance.
  • Interior Angle — The angle measured inside the traverse polygon at each station. For a polygon traversed anticlockwise, interior angles are on the left; for clockwise traversal, on the right.
  • Bearing — The direction of a line expressed as an angle from North, measured clockwise. Range: 0° to 360°. Also called whole-circle bearing (WCB).
  • Latitude (L) — The north-south component of a traverse leg. L = D × cos(bearing). Positive = northing (N); negative = southing (S).
  • Departure (D) — The east-west component of a traverse leg. Dep = D × sin(bearing). Positive = easting (E); negative = westing (W).
  • Closing Error — In a closed traverse, the sum of all latitudes and sum of all departures should each equal zero. Any non-zero sum is the closing error.
  • Accuracy Ratio — The closing error as a fraction of the total traverse length: 1 : (Perimeter / CE). Higher ratio = more accurate.
  • Bowditch Adjustment — The most common method of distributing the closing error proportionally across all traverse legs based on their length.
"The closing error is not a failure — it is a measurement. It tells you how good your fieldwork was, and the adjustment distributes that small imperfection systematically."

Step 1 — Check the Interior Angles

For a closed polygon traverse with n stations, the sum of the interior angles must equal a specific value. This is the first arithmetic check, done before any bearing or coordinate calculation:

Interior Angle Sum Check
Sum of interior angles = (n − 2) × 180°
Where n = number of sides (= number of stations). For a 4-sided traverse: (4−2)×180 = 360°. For a 5-sided: 540°. For a 6-sided: 720°. If the measured sum differs, distribute the angular error equally across all angles.

If the sum of measured interior angles differs from the theoretical sum, the difference is the angular misclosure. For ordinary engineering work, this should be within ±1' × √n (one minute per station). If it is larger, re-measure the suspect angles.

Step 2 — Compute Bearings from Angles

Once angles are adjusted, compute the bearing of each leg. The rule for computing bearings from interior angles depends on the direction of traversal:

Bearing Computation (Interior Angles, Clockwise Traversal)
Forward bearing = Back bearing + 180° − Interior Angle
If result > 360°: subtract 360°. If result < 0°: add 360°. The "back bearing" of leg AB = bearing of BA = (bearing AB + 180°), adjusted if >360°.

Step 3 — Calculate Latitudes and Departures

For each leg, the latitude (northing component) and departure (easting component) are calculated from the leg length and bearing:

Latitude and Departure
Latitude = Distance × cos(Bearing) Departure = Distance × sin(Bearing)
Bearing must be in degrees. The signs come out automatically from the trig functions — cos and sin handle North/South and East/West signs correctly as long as you use the whole-circle bearing (0–360°).

Step 4 — Calculate Closing Error and Accuracy

In a perfect traverse, all the latitudes would sum to exactly zero, and all departures would sum to exactly zero. In practice, there is always a small discrepancy. This is the closing error:

Closing Error and Accuracy Ratio
ΣL = sum of all latitudes (should be 0) ΣD = sum of all departures (should be 0) Linear closing error: CE = √(ΣL² + ΣD²) Accuracy ratio: 1 : (Perimeter / CE)
The accuracy ratio is expressed as "1 in X" — for example, 1:5000 means the error is 1 unit per 5000 units of total traverse length. Engineering surveys typically require 1:2000 to 1:10000. Geodetic surveys require 1:50000 or better.

Step 5 — The Bowditch Adjustment

The Bowditch Rule (also called the Compass Rule) is the most widely used method of distributing the closing error across all legs. It assumes that both distance measurement and angle measurement contribute equally to the error — so the correction to each leg is proportional to its length.

Bowditch (Compass Rule) Adjustment
Correction to Latitude (leg i) = −ΣL × (length_i / Perimeter) Correction to Departure (leg i) = −ΣD × (length_i / Perimeter)
Apply these corrections to each leg's latitude and departure. The corrected latitudes should then sum to exactly zero, and corrected departures should sum to exactly zero. This is your second arithmetic check.
Bowditch vs Transit Rule

The Bowditch Rule weights corrections by leg length — best when distance and angle errors are roughly equal (typical for total station work). The Transit Rule weights by the magnitude of the latitude or departure — better when angles are much more accurate than distances (theodolite + tape work). For modern total station traverses, Bowditch is the standard.

Step 6 — Compute Final Coordinates

With corrected latitudes and departures, calculate the E,N coordinates of each station by cumulative addition from the known starting point:

Coordinate Computation
E_(i+1) = E_i + Corrected Departure (leg i→i+1) N_(i+1) = N_i + Corrected Latitude (leg i→i+1)
Starting from the known coordinates of station A, apply each corrected departure and latitude in sequence to get coordinates of B, C, D… The final station's computed coordinates must match the known coordinates of A (for a closed loop). This is your final check.

Final Check — Closure on A

The computed coordinates of A at the end must equal the known starting coordinates of A exactly. If they do, the Bowditch corrections were applied correctly. If not, find the arithmetic error before using the coordinates for any setting-out work.

Traverse Accuracy Standards

Knowing your accuracy ratio is only useful if you know what is acceptable. Here are the standard accuracy requirements used in engineering and cadastral surveying:

Survey TypeMinimum Accuracy RatioTypical Use
Third Order1 : 2 000General construction, earthworks control
Second Order1 : 5 000Engineering structures, road alignment
First Order1 : 10 000Primary control, cadastral surveys
High Precision1 : 25 000Geodetic work, dam monitoring
Geodetic1 : 100 000+National control networks, GNSS check
When Accuracy is Below Minimum

If your accuracy ratio falls below the required standard, do not adjust and move on. Go back to the field. Re-measure the longest legs first (they contribute most to error), re-measure any angles that were difficult to observe, and check for errors in booking. A bad traverse that gets adjusted and used on site causes expensive setting-out errors that are very difficult to diagnose later.

The 7-Step Sequence — Quick Reference

01
Measure and book field data

Record all interior angles and horizontal distances. Note which direction the traverse runs (clockwise or anticlockwise). Record starting coordinates and starting bearing.

02
Check angular sum

Sum interior angles. Compare with (n−2)×180°. If angular misclosure exists and is within tolerance, distribute equally. If outside tolerance, re-measure suspect angles.

03
Compute all bearings

Using the starting bearing and adjusted interior angles: forward bearing = back bearing + 180° − interior angle. Adjust results into 0°–360° range.

04
Calculate latitudes and departures

For each leg: Latitude = D × cos(bearing), Departure = D × sin(bearing). Build the table with all legs.

05
Sum and assess closing error

ΣL and ΣD give the lat and dep closing errors. CE = √(ΣL² + ΣD²). Accuracy = 1:(Perimeter/CE). Compare with the required standard.

06
Apply Bowditch adjustment

Correction to each leg: CL_i = −ΣL × (d_i/P), CD_i = −ΣD × (d_i/P). Add corrections to raw lat and dep. Verify corrected sums are zero.

07
Compute final coordinates

Start from known A coordinates. Add corrected departures and latitudes sequentially. Verify final return to A matches exactly.

Summary

A closed loop traverse is one of the fundamental skills of practical surveying. The measurement is straightforward — angles and distances. The calculation follows a fixed sequence that every surveyor can learn and apply consistently. And the self-checking nature of the closed loop — where the closing error tells you exactly how well the work was done — makes it one of the most reliable methods of establishing a coordinate control network.

Remember the sequence: check angles → compute bearings → calculate latitudes and departures → assess closing error and accuracy → apply Bowditch → compute final coordinates → verify closure. Do not skip any step. The checks at each stage protect you from errors that would otherwise only surface when setting-out work reveals a mismatch between theory and ground.
