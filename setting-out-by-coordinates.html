Setting Out by Coordinates:
A Step-by-Step Guide

Setting out is the reverse of surveying — instead of measuring where things are, you place things where they need to be. With a total station and a list of design coordinates, you can stake any point on a construction site to millimetre precision. This article explains exactly how.

Akhtar Engineering Hub 3 May 2026 10 min read Field Practice

Every peg, every column, every manhole cover and every road kerb on a construction site was placed by a surveyor using setting out. It is the bridge between engineering drawings — full of coordinates, levels and dimensions — and physical reality in the ground. Without it, nothing gets built in the right place.

Modern total stations make coordinate setting out fast and systematic. You enter the target coordinates, the instrument tells your prism man which direction to move and by how much, and when the display reads zero — you mark the point. This article walks through the complete process from preparation to final peg.

What This Article Covers

Setting out vs surveying — prerequisites and preparation — the stake-out screen explained — the three-stage approach process — face left vs face right — setting out levels — tolerances for different work types — a column grid worked example — and quality control checks.

Setting Out vs Surveying — The Opposite Process

📡
Surveying

Ground → Coordinates

Measure an existing point. Record angles and distances. Compute its E,N,H coordinates. The point already exists — you are finding its position.

⇄
📍
Setting Out

Coordinates → Ground

You have a target E,N,H from the design. You physically place a peg or mark at exactly that position. The point does not yet exist — you are creating it.

The mathematics is the same in both directions — bearings, distances, angles and levels. The difference is only in what is known and what is unknown. In surveying you know the ground position and calculate the coordinates. In setting out you know the coordinates and calculate how to reach the ground position.

"Every coordinate on a design drawing is a promise. Setting out is how you keep that promise — converting numbers on paper into marks on the ground that the construction team can build to."

Prerequisites Before You Start

Good setting out begins before you pick up the instrument. These are the things to have in order:

  • Design coordinates list: A table of all points to be set out with their E, N and H values. This should come from the engineer or designer — never scale coordinates from a drawing
  • Verified control network: Your control points must be in the same coordinate system as the design. If the design uses a site grid and your control is in national coordinates, a transformation is needed
  • Instrument set up and verified: Either on a known peg with backsight, or using a free station resection (see the resection article). Residual checked and within tolerance
  • Correct prism height: The prism pole must be set to a known height (typically 2.000 m) and consistent throughout the session. Any change in prism height during the job must be re-entered in the instrument
  • Clear communication: Radio or phone contact with the prism man. Agree on your direction commands before starting — left/right, forward/back, up/down in consistent units
  • Marking materials: Pegs, stakes, spray paint, nails, chalk — whatever is appropriate for the work and surface

The Stake-Out Screen Explained

Every total station has a stake-out (or setting out) mode. The display shows the difference between where the prism currently is and where it needs to be. Here is what the typical stake-out screen shows and what each value means:

  • dRight (+) / dLeft (−) — How far the prism needs to move perpendicular to the line of sight. Positive = move the prism to your right (the instrument operator's right). The prism man moves in the opposite direction — this is a common source of confusion, always agree on conventions first
  • dForward (+) / dBack (−) — How far the prism needs to move along the line of sight — toward or away from the instrument. Positive = prism needs to move away from the instrument (forward)
  • dH / Cut / Fill — The height difference between the prism's current measured height and the target design level. Cut = prism is above design level (ground needs to be cut). Fill = prism is below design level (ground needs to be filled or raised)
  • Horizontal Distance — The measured horizontal distance from the instrument to the prism. Useful for sanity-checking that you are near the right area
When All Three Values Read Zero

When dRight, dForward and dH all read 0.000 (or within your tolerance), the prism is at the design point. Mark the ground directly below the prism pole tip. Double-check by taking a second measurement before marking permanently.

The Three-Stage Approach Process

Getting from "somewhere near the right area" to "exactly on point" follows a consistent three-stage process. Experienced prism men know this intuitively — teaching it explicitly to new operators saves significant time on site.

Stage 1 — Rough
> 0.5 m

Walk to approximately the right position. Use the horizontal distance on the screen as a guide. The instrument operator signals large movements — 5 m left, 10 m forward. Get into the ballpark quickly, do not waste time shuffling at this stage.

Stage 2 — Fine
0.05–0.5 m

Start using the dRight and dForward values. Move methodically — one direction at a time. First get dForward to near zero (line of sight direction), then move laterally to get dRight to zero. Take a measurement after each move.

Stage 3 — Mark
< 5 mm

When within 5–10 mm, make tiny adjustments. Hold the pole vertical (check the circular bubble), take a final measurement, confirm all three values are within tolerance, and mark. Take a second independent measurement to verify before driving a peg.

Fig 1. The three-stage approach. Stage 1 (red): rough positioning by walking. Stage 2 (amber): fine adjustment using dRight and dForward on the screen. Stage 3 (green): within tolerance — verify and mark.

Tolerances — What is Acceptable?

Setting-out tolerance depends entirely on what is being set out. A foundation pile position has a completely different tolerance requirement to a road centreline peg. Here are the typical standards used on construction projects:

The table above shows typical industry values, but every contract has its own specification. Always check the project's Surveying or Setting Out specification — it defines the legal tolerance you are working to. If no specification exists, use the tightest value above for the work type and document your method.

Face Left and Face Right — When It Matters

For most construction setting out, a single face observation is sufficient — especially with a modern total station that has a compensator correcting for small levelling errors. However, for high-precision structural work (columns, piles, critical alignment), observing in both face left and face right eliminates collimation error and gives you a higher-quality result:

  1. Observe the prism in face left. Note the displayed dRight and dForward values
  2. Transit the instrument (rotate 180° in horizontal, flip the telescope vertically)
  3. Observe the same prism in face right. Note the new values
  4. The mean of the face left and face right positions is the corrected point location
  5. Use the mean values to make the final mark
When to Use Double-Face Setting Out

Use both faces when: the required tolerance is 5 mm or tighter, the line of sight is longer than 100 m, the instrument has not been recently calibrated, or you are setting out a point that cannot be checked later (e.g., a pile before pouring). For earthworks and road pegging, single face is standard.

Setting Out Levels

The dH (height difference) shown in the stake-out screen is computed from the instrument's measured elevation to the prism tip, corrected for prism height, compared against the design level. This means the total station can set out levels as well as plan position simultaneously — a major advantage over using a separate level instrument.

Stake-Out Level Calculation
Measured H at prism = H_instrument + ΔH (from EDM vertical angle) dH = H_design − H_prism_current Positive dH → CUT (ground too high, needs to come down) Negative dH → FILL (ground too low, needs material added)
The instrument height (HI) must be entered correctly — measured from ground mark to the trunnion axis of the instrument. The prism height (HR) must also be set correctly and consistently on the pole. An error in either propagates directly into every level set out from that station.

Quality Control — Four Checks That Protect You

Independent Re-Observation

After setting out a point, move to a different instrument station (or change the instrument orientation) and re-observe the mark. If it reads within tolerance from the new position, it is correct. Discrepancies between the two observations flag errors in setup or marking.

Check Tape Measurement

For column grids and linear structures, check key dimensions by tape after setting out. Grid spacings, overall dimensions and diagonal checks are fast and catch gross errors. A 6.000 m spacing that measures 5.987 m is immediately obvious and requires investigation.

As-Built Survey

Survey the set-out marks as a check survey — measure them back as if they were existing features and compare to the design coordinates. This produces a formal as-built table showing the error at each point. Required on most building and infrastructure contracts.

Level Check by Spirit Level

For precise level setting out, always verify critical levels using a conventional automatic level and staff — independent of the total station. This cross-checks that the instrument's HI and prism heights were entered correctly and that the EDM height calculation is working properly.

Common Errors That Waste Hours

  • Wrong coordinate system: Design is in site grid, control is in national coordinates, no transformation applied. Everything is consistently wrong in the same direction — easy to miss. Always confirm the coordinate system match before starting
  • Transposed E and N: Entering N where E should be. The point stakes out in a completely different location. Catching this requires a sanity check — does the horizontal distance to the first point make sense given where it should be?
  • Prism height change not re-entered: The prism pole slips from 2.000 m to 1.850 m mid-job. All subsequent level readings are 150 mm wrong. Always check the pole height visually before each observation series
  • Instrument moved mid-session: Someone kicks the tripod. The station coordinates are now wrong. All subsequent setting out from that setup is invalid. Always re-verify the setup after any suspected instrument disturbance
  • Not marking below the pole: The prism is on point but the mark is made offset from the pole base. The prism pole must be held vertically (circular bubble centred) and the mark made directly below the pole tip — not below the prism itself
  • Not recording the work: No as-built table, no sketch, no photographs. If a dispute arises later, you cannot prove what was set out or when. Keep field notes of every setting-out session

Summary

Setting out by coordinates is the daily bread of construction surveying. The total station does the geometry for you — your job is to ensure the instrument is set up correctly, the coordinates are right, the prism height is consistent, and the approach to each point is systematic. Then protect the work with proper quality control checks before handing it over to the construction team.

The three-stage approach (rough → fine → mark) is the most efficient process regardless of how complex the site. The tolerance table keeps you honest about what is and is not acceptable for each type of work. And the four QC checks — independent re-observation, tape check, as-built survey, and level verification — are what separate professional setting out from guesswork.

Combined with the earlier articles in this series on free station setup and traverse calculations, you now have the complete picture of how a professional survey team establishes control, sets up the instrument, and translates design coordinates into physical marks on the ground.
